CAiSE’02 Tutorial

 

Title:                               Fact-oriented modeling

Presenter:                       Terry Halpin, Microsoft Corporation

Duration:                         3 hours

Intended audience:          Practitioners or academics with an interest in data modeling.

Background required:     Basic understanding of relational databases, and at least one data modeling approach (e.g. ER, or UML class modeling)

Abstract:

Effective business rules management, data warehousing, enterprise modeling and re-engineering all depend on the quality of the underlying data model. To properly exploit database technology, a clear understanding is needed as to how to create conceptual business models, transform them to logical database models for implementation on the chosen platform, and query the populated models. Fact-orientation provides a truly conceptual way to accomplish these tasks, facilitating communication between the modeler and the domain expert. This tutorial provides an overview of the fact-oriented approach for modeling and querying information systems, and compares it with other data modeling methods such as object-oriented and entity-relationship approaches. While fact-orientation can be used independently of other data modeling techniques, it can also be used productively as a front-end to them, since it performs conceptual data analysis at a higher level and enables a wider range of business rules to be visualized graphically.

One main aim of this tutorial is to provide an up-to-date overview of Object-Role Modeling (ORM), a fact-oriented approach to information modeling that offers many benefits over other data modeling approaches. Originating in Europe in the early 70s, ORM was designed from the ground up to provide a conceptual approach to information modeling that is:

·        Understandable (facts and rules are expressible in natural language or intuitive graphics)

·        Reliable   (business rules are validated using natural language and sample populations)

·        Capable (many business rules can be captured graphically)

·        Stable (application domain changes have minimal impact on ORM models or queries)

·        Executable (ORM’s graphical language can be automatically mapped to DDL code)

Over the last 30 years, ORM has been extended in many ways, and is now supported by various modeling tools. This presentation covers the basic concepts underlying modern ORM, as well as various techniques used in ORM to create or transform models. The main topics covered are:

·        fact mining— going from data (syntax) to facts (semantics -- the meaning of the data)

·        rule mining—determining constraints on fact populations, and rules for deriving new facts

·        verbalizing and validating business rules using data use cases

·        comparing ORM with ER and UML

·        metamodeling issues for ORM, ER and UML

·        mapping conceptual to relational schemas

·        optimizing schemas through conceptual transformations

·        querying databases at a conceptual level

Techniques such as harvesting data use cases for fact structures and business rules are useful, whatever data modeling notation is used. However, ORM makes it much easier to master these techniques than other approaches such as Entity Relationship (ER) modeling, or UML class modeling. ORM’s attribute-free approach enables natural verbalization of data models, and its rich, orthogonal notation enables many more business rules to be captured simply and formally. Since ORM models map in a fairly transparent way to UML and ER models, they can be used in conjunction with them if desired.

When seeding the data model with data use cases, information can be presented in many different ways (tables, graphs, forms, maps etc.), and practice is needed in verbalizing these different visualizations to arrive at the underlying conceptual facts. The tutorial utilizes a variety of examples to promote the required verbalization skills. It also shows how verbalization of facts and rules, in conjunction with positive and negative examples, can be optimally used to validate business rules, exploiting both positive and negative verbalizations of the rules. The different roles played by modeler and subject matter expert in the modeling process are also highlighted. Rule visualizations in UML, ER and ORM are illustrated and compared so that users of any of these notations can make use of the principles discussed.

Bio:

 

Dr. Terry Halpin, BSc, DipEd, BA, MLitStud, PhD, is Program Manager in Database Modeling, Enterprise Framework and Tools Unit, at Microsoft Corporation in Redmond, Washington, USA. After a lengthy career as an academic in computer science, which he combined with industrial work on database modeling, he moved to industry full-time, heading database research at Asymetrix Corporation, InfoModelers Inc., and Visio Corporation before its acquisition by Microsoft. For many years, his research has focused on conceptual modeling and conceptual query technology for information systems, using a business rules approach. His doctoral thesis formalized Object-Role Modeling (ORM/NIAM), and his publications include over ninety technical papers, and four books, including Information Modeling and Relational Databases (2001: Morgan Kaufmann). He is a member of IFIP WG 8.1 (Design and Evaluation of Information Systems), and has presented at dozens of international conferences in both industry and academia.
